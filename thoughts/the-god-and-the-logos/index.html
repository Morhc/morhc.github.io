The God and the Logos

Posted on April 30, 2026

A lot of Christians who have done some study of the gospel of John are aware of that the author of the opening hymn (note that John is a composite text from many editors) directly borrows the concept of the logos from Greek philosophy.

John 1.1-2 (David Bentley Hart's second edition): In the origin there was the Logos, and the Logos was present to God, and the Logos was god; this one was present to God in the origin.

Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος οὗτος ἦν ἐν ἀρχῇ πρὸς τὸν θεόν

For many, seeing that the Logos being called god rather than God constitutes directly blasphemy, but there is a very good reason for translating the text of John 1 this way.

...the wording here seems intentionally elliptical, so as to avoid speaking of the Logos in a way that, in late antique usage, was reserved properly for the Father... I adopt the typographical convention of the capital G followed by small capitals to indicate where the Greek speaks of ὁ θεός (ho theos), which clearly means God in the fullest and most unequivocal sense, whereas I confine myself entirely to lowercase letters to indicate where the Greek speaks only of θεός (theos) without the article... which would suggest at least a pictorial continuity with a tradition of seeing the Logos or “secondary god” as the heavenly high priest, turned toward the unknowable Father and leading the worship of God by all rational creatures.
— David Bentley Hart, Explanatory note footnoted on John 1.1

This [using a lowercase g for θεός], hesitantly, is how I deal with the distinction in my translation of the Gospel's prologue, and I believe one must employ some such device: it seems to me that the withholding of the full revelation of Christ as ho theos, God in the fullest sense, until Thomas confesses him as such in the light of Easter, must be seen as an intentional authorial tactic. Some other scholars have chosen to render the inarticular form of theos as “a divine being,” but this seems wrong to me on two counts: first, if that were all the evangelist were saying, he could have used the perfectly serviceable Greek word theios; and, second, the text clearly means to assert some kind of continuity of divinity between God the Father and the Logos, not merely some sort of association between “God proper” and “a divine being.” Here, I take it, one may regard chapter twenty as providing the ultimate interpretation of chapter one, and allow one's translation to reflect that.
— David Bentley Hart, A Note on the Prologue of John's Gospel

I want to challenge David's affirmation that the gospel of John is intentionally trying to portray Jesus as ὁ θεός and wait until John 20 to fully unveil that to the reader. He is aware of much more scholarship than I, much better read in the patristics and ancient Greek literature, and can actually read these texts in the original language. However, despite all his bravado about skirting the theologically acceptable and staking his claims on his Vedantic Christianity (which has been quite disappointing to me so far and feels like cultural appropriation rather than a truly syncretic theology). Whatever is happening in John 20, I don't think it is correct for David to say that this is "pictorial continuity with a tradition of the logos as a secondary god" or that the author of the hymn should've used other means if they intended to clearly portray the Logos as a divine being and not in a unique co-substantial relationship with the Father.

Point the first: the gospel of John is probably ripping the concept of logos straight from Philo. Philo, a Jewish philospher and theologian living in Alexandria who died in 50 AD or so, found a way to syncretize Jewish beliefs with Greek philosophy to read the Hebrew Scriptures allegorically. In his work On Dreams, he discusses how many in the Hebrew Scriptures are given the title of θεός despite not being the God of gods Adonai. His reasoning will immediately ring in our ears when we compare it to the gospel of John.

For it is said: "I am the God who was seen by thee;" not in my place, but in the place of God, as if he meant of some other God. What then ought we to say? There is one true God only: but they who are called Gods, by an abuse of language, are numerous; on which account the holy scripture on the present occasion indicates that it is the true God that is meant by the use of the article, the expression being, "I am the God [ho Theos, ὁ θεός];" but when the word is used incorrectly, it is put without the article, the expression being, "He who was seen by thee in the place," not of the God [tou Theou, τὸν θεοῦ], but simply "of God" [Theou, θεοῦ]; and what he here calls God is his most ancient word, not having any superstitious regard to the position of the names, but only proposing one end to himself, namely, to give a true account of the matter; for in other passages the sacred historian, when he considered whether there really was any name belonging to the living God, showed that he knew that there was none properly belonging to him; but that whatever appellation any one may give him, will be an abuse of terms; for the living God is not of a nature to be described, but only to be.
— Philo of Alexandria, On Dreams I.228-230

Why is it that he speaks as if of some other god, saying that he made man after the image of God, and not that he made him after his own image? [refering to Genesis 9.6]. Very appropriately and without any falsehood was this oracular sentence uttered by God, for no mortal thing could have been formed on the similitude of the supreme Father of the universe, but only after the pattern of the second deity, who is the Word [ὁ λόγος] of the supreme Being; since it is fitting that the rational soul of man should bear it the type of the divine Word; since in his first Word God is superior to the most rational possible nature. But he who is superior to the Word holds his rank in a better and most singular pre-eminence, and how could the creature possibly exhibit a likeness of him in himself? Nevertheless he also wished to intimate this fact, that God does rightly and correctly require vengeance, in order to the defence of virtuous and consistent men, because such bear in themselves a familiar acquaintance with his Word, of which the human mind is the similitude and form.
— Philo of Alexandria, Questions and Answers on Genesis II.62

What is Philo saying here? In the Hebrew Scriptures there are many beings called god, but are not the God. Obviously he is uncomfortable with this (calling it an abuse of language), but he feels like he has to accommodate it rather than disagree with the text. He does this by pointing out that these beings are not called the God, but are called god without the explicit qualifier. This is what is seen in the opening hymn where the logos is called θεὸς and God is called τὸν θεόν. Further, he clearly argues that the logos is a secondary, subservient god to the true God. No one could have been fashioned in the image of the supreme Father, so we must have been fashioned after the logos (who is the most exalted of God's creation) and hence why the LXX translates "image of God" in Genesis 9.6 as "ἐν εἰκόνι θεοῦ" and not "ἐν εἰκόνι τὸν θεοῦ". To me, this alone is sufficient to close the question whether the opening of John portrays the logos as a divine being or God proper. The author is clearly not allergic to using the article for the logos (ὁ λόγος) but does not use it when calling the logos "god". But let's see a bit of Christian reception history.

Point the second: Justin Martyr, who is one of the earliest Christians who we have writings from (died 165 AD) also argues this. Justin, who may or may not have read the gospel of John (and even if he did, what form it was in we do not know), definitely has a logos Christology and understands it in the same terms as Philo.

I shall attempt to persuade you, since you have understood the Scriptures, [of the truth] of what I say, that there is, and that there is said to be, another God and Lord subject to the Maker of all things; who is also called an Angel, because He announces to men whatsoever the Maker of all things — above whom there is no other God — wishes to announce to them.

These and other such sayings are recorded by the lawgiver and by the prophets; and I suppose that I have stated sufficiently, that wherever God [ὁ θεός] says, 'God [ὁ θεός] went up from Abraham,' [Genesis 18.22] or, 'The Lord spoke to Moses,' [Exodus 6.29] and 'The Lord came down to behold the tower which the sons of men had built,' [Genesis 11.5] or when 'God [ὁ θεός] shut Noah into the ark,' [Genesis 7.16] you must not imagine that the unbegotten God [τόν ἀγέννητος θεὸν] Himself came down or went up from any place... neither Abraham, nor Isaac, nor Jacob, nor any other man, saw the Father and ineffable Lord of all, and also of Christ, but [saw] Him who was according to His will His Son, being God [θεός], and the Angel because He ministered to His will; whom also it pleased Him to be born man by the Virgin; who also was fire when He conversed with Moses from the bush.
— Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho 56, 127

Our teacher of these things is Jesus Christ, who also was born for this purpose, and was crucified under Pontius Pilate, procurator of Judæa, in the times of Tiberius Cæsar; and that we reasonably worship Him, having learned that He is the Son of the true God Himself, and holding Him in the second place, and the prophetic Spirit in the third, we will prove. For they proclaim our madness to consist in this, that we give to a crucified man a place second to the unchangeable and eternal God, the Creator of all; for they do not discern the mystery that is herein, to which, as we make it plain to you, we pray you to give heed.
— Justin Martyr, First Apology 13

Justin's theology clearly is comfortable enough, like Philo, to make reference to Christ as another God or a second God that is not Himself the completely transcendent Father but instead at a second place to the eternal God and Creator of all. Justin is contemporary with the writing of the gospel of John and so if he is thinking clearly in these ways, I think it is appropriate to say that the gospel could have easily been doing the same thing. When referencing Scripture that says ὁ θεός, Justin does not quibble like Philo, but goes on to call Jesus θεός without the article. It seems extremely unlikely that Justin goes through all this effort to call Christ a second God if he was in agreement with later Trinitarian doctrine.

Point the third: Origen of Alexandria's (died 253 AD) commentary of the gospel of John who is very in tune with the philosophical milleu of his day, also reads this passage the same way and takes care to distinguish between God and the logos. I think it is worth reading this passage in its entirety because Origen's commentary is actually so clear on his reading of John.

But since the proposition, "In the beginning was the Word," has been placed first, perhaps it indicates some order; in the same manner, next, "And the Word was with God," and third, "And the Word was God." Perhaps he says, "And the Word was with God," then, "And the Word was God," that we might understand that the Word has become God because he is "with God." John has used the articles in one place and omitted them in another very precisely, and not as though he did not understand the precision of the Greek language. In the case of the Word, he adds the article "the," but in the case of the noun "God," he inserts it in one place and omits it in another. For he adds the article when the noun "God" stands for the uncreated cause of the universe, but he omits it when the Word is referred to as "God." And as "the God" and "God" differ in these places, so, perhaps, "the Word" and "Word" differ. For as the God who is over all is "the God" and not simply "God," so the source of reason in each rational being is "the Word." That reason which is in each rational being would not properly have the same designation as the first reason, and be said to be "the Word." Many people who wish to be pious are troubled because they are afraid that they may proclaim two Gods and, for this reason, they fall into false and impious beliefs. They either deny that the individual nature of the Son is other than that of the Father by confessing him to be God whom they refer to as "Son" in name at least, or they deny the divinity of the Son and make his individual nature and essence as an individual to be different from the Father. Their problem can be resolved in this way. We must say to them that at one time God, with the article, is very God, wherefore also the Savior says in his prayer to the Father, "That they may know you the only true God." On the other hand, everything besides the very God, which is made God by participation in his divinity, would more properly not be said to be "the God," but "God." To be sure, his "firstborn of every creature," inasmuch as he was the first to be with God and has drawn divinity into himself, is more honored than the other gods beside him (of whom God is God as it is said, "The God of gods, the Lord has spoken, and he has called the earth"). It was by his ministry that they became gods, for he drew from God that they might be deified, sharing ungrudgingly also with them according to his goodness. T`he God, therefore, is the true God. The others are gods formed according to him as images of the prototype. But again, the archetypal image of the many images is the Word with the God, who was "in the beginning." By being "with the God" he always continues to be "God." But he would not have this if he were not with God, and he would not remain God if he did not continue in unceasing contemplation of the depth of the Father.
— Origen of Alexandria, Commentary on the Gospel According to John II.12-18

Origen here is clearly reflecting the language of Philo as he is reading these opening verses. He may be the Church's first exegete ever and here he is saying explicitly that John 1.1-2 is not calling the Logos God, but a lower divinity. Now, of course, the Logos is showered with high praises throughout all of Origen's theology. He distinguishes so clearly here that the Logos is not the God who created all things, but the divine principle of reason in all of us. The Logos is God to the extent that the Logos is with God, participating in divinity. That is the same, by the way, with human beings. We too can be properly called God when we participate in the divine essence, just as the Logos. The Logos is distinct from us because He is the firstborn through whom all things were made, including ourselves, but Origen does not elevate the Logos to the status of the God. In fact, he says that if the Logos stopped being "with" God that He would no longer be called "God".

I think that this demonstrates that the gospel of John very clearly does depict the logos as a secondary divine being without ambiguity. What happens later in the gospel may have room for debate, but I think that David is far too cautious in his translation of the gospel and that his explanatory notes tiptoe around the problem. I think that the discussions about the logos and calling others than the divine Creator god pre-Nicea shows that it is more correct to translate the text that the Logos was a divine being, a secondary god, or a lower divinity. I don't think it is appropriate for David to use the rhetorical flourish of "pictorial continuity with a tradition" that is effectively a wink at the reader that what John really means is that the logos is God proper in the Nicene sense. Rather, I think that John dropping the article is a clear indication to the philosophically minded reader that the Logos is not the God, but a god. This was not lost on Origen, and should not be lost on us either.

What does this say for Nicene theology and later developed doctrine? David's response (in Tradition and Apocalypse) is that while Arius is in continuity with the traditional way of thinking about the relationship between the Logos and the Father, the Nicene fathers innovated correctly to say that the Logos is of the same essence with the Father. Now he sustains this by his monistic theology (proposed in You Are Gods and The Light of Tabor) that all things are participating in divinity and so all things can be called God. The average reading is that Christ (and the Spirit) being of the same being with the Father (true God from true God in the Creed) is to also say that it is a way of being that is not shared by creation. I am unsure that the Nicene creed is in agreement with David's theology (or Origen's or John's for that matter). I think the notes here in the second edition of the translation of the New Testament from him (published between You Are Gods and The Light of Tabor) really tries to play both sides of the debate at the same time.

Where does that leave us? For myself, I am more interested in the living tradition rather than texts. I am a Christian independent of the authorial intent of the Biblical authors. They were trying to communicate something about the person they encountered, and the living tradition has done the same. The same applies to the creeds. So I affirm the creed, but when asked I will say that Jesus was and is and ever will be One who fully presenced God to us.
