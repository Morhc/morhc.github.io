John, Herod, & the Prophetic Critique

Posted on June 6, 2026

When I was thinking about cannibalism, I noticed something else that is also interesting.

Mark 6.17-26 For Herod himself had sent men who arrested John, bound him, and put him in prison on account of Herodias, his brother Philip's wife, because Herod had married her. For John had been telling Herod, “It is not lawful for you to have your brother's wife.” And Herodias had a grudge against him and wanted to kill him. But she could not, for Herod feared John, knowing that he was a righteous and holy man, and he protected him. When he heard him, he was greatly perplexed, and yet he liked to listen to him. But an opportunity came when Herod on his birthday gave a banquet for his courtiers and officers and for the leaders of Galilee. When his daughter Herodias came in and danced, she pleased Herod and his guests, and the king said to the girl, “Ask me for whatever you wish, and I will give it.” And he swore to her, “Whatever you ask me, I will give you, even half of my kingdom.” She went out and said to her mother, “What should I ask for?” She replied, “The head of John the baptizer.” Immediately she rushed back to the king and requested, “I want you to give me at once the head of John the Baptist on a platter.” The king was deeply grieved, yet out of regard for his oaths and for the guests, he did not want to refuse her.

Herod enjoyed listening to John's message? And was deeply grieved at having to behead him? Not scared because he was so holy and righteous (something he knew), or scared of the crowds, or something other than being deeply grieved. How well did they know each other? Did John regularly visit Herod? Did Herod regularly summon John? I can't imagine Herod going to the dessert, and it doesn't sound like he just sent some courtesan to report to him. It sounds like Herod and John had some regular, ongoing relationship. Is the gospel of Mark constructing this story? Or is it reflecting some historical kernel of the John the baptist tradition. Is the relationship between John and Herod getting modelled on the ancient prophets who critiqued the king, like Ahab and Elijah? But like a positive prophetic critique? You even have the critique of the wife via Herodias/Jezebel.

Interestingly, Josephus says that Herod was scared of John's power over the power so he put him down (reflected in Matthew 14.5; Luke 3.19-20 says because John was rebuking him over the marriage).

Now many people came in crowds to him, for they were greatly moved by his words. Herod, who feared that the great influence John had over the masses might put them into his power and enable him to raise a rebellion (for they seemed ready to do anything he should advise), thought it best to put him to death. In this way, he might prevent any mischief John might cause, and not bring himself into difficulties by sparing a man who might make him repent of it when it would be too late.
— Josephus, Jewish Antiquities 18.118

Maybe the gospel was intentionally copying the prophetic critique model, but making it more positive because Herod was associated with Rome and the gospel wanted to cozy up to Rome? And the historical kernel is that Herod was a malicious, blood-soaken, power-hungry guy who put down dangerous, radical prophets?

Luke 3.10-14 And the crowds asked him, “What, then, should we do?” In reply he said to them, “Whoever has two coats must share with anyone who has none, and whoever has food must do likewise.” Even tax collectors came to be baptized, and they asked him, “Teacher, what should we do?” He said to them, “Collect no more than the amount prescribed for you.” Soldiers also asked him, “And we, what should we do?” He said to them, “Do not extort money from anyone by threats or false accusation, and be satisfied with your wages.”

Mutual aid and all that. Rest In Power John.
