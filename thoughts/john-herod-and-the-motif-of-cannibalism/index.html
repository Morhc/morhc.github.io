John, Herod, & the Motif of Cannibalism

Posted on May 11, 2026

I noticed something somewhat strange in Mark's telling of the fate of John the baptist.

Mark 6.21-29 But an opportunity came when Herod on his birthday gave a banquet for his courtiers and officers and for the leaders of Galilee. When his daughter Herodias came in and danced, she pleased Herod and his guests, and the king said to the girl, “Ask me for whatever you wish, and I will give it.” And he swore to her, “Whatever you ask me, I will give you, even half of my kingdom.” She went out and said to her mother, “What should I ask for?” She replied, “The head of John the baptizer.” Immediately she rushed back to the king and requested, “I want you to give me at once the head of John the Baptist on a platter.” The king was deeply grieved, yet out of regard for his oaths and for the guests, he did not want to refuse her. Immediately the king sent a soldier of the guard with orders to bring John's head. He went and beheaded him in the prison, brought his head on a platter, and gave it to the girl. Then the girl gave it to her mother. When his disciples heard about it, they came and took his body and laid it in a tomb.

Is it just me, or is anyone else getting strong cannibalism vibes from this one. Herod is hosting a dinner party where the head of John the baptist gets served on a platter, and when John's disciples come they bury the body, but no mention of the fate of the head. This story is then immediately followed by Jesus feeding five thousand men. There are two eating scenes going on in this section of Mark and one missing head. Is it possible that the author of this gospel wants us to take away the implication of cannibalism?

I was looking around for any scholarship that noticed this, and found an article named Fish Tales written by Alicia J. Batten and published in Biblical Theology Bulletin. She is an associate professor at Conrad Grebel for religious studies, so shoutout to the homies. She points out that there are clear linguistic connections in Greek that juxtapose Herod's dinner party with the symposium where five thousand men are fed. Rightly, she pulls at the class implications in the text, comparing the wealthy Herodian party with lay people eating from the hand of Jesus. Because she's focused on the demographics of who eat fish, she does not dwell on the story too long, but I see this as evidence that some people Biblical scholarship may see some connection between John's head and the act of eating.

Also, as an aside, she provides this fantastic reading of the feeding that I absolutely adore.

The text does not say that Jesus multiplied the two fish, but simply that the two fish were enough for 5000 people to consume. This suggests that somehow they were turned into massive fish.
— Alicia J. Batten, Fish Tales

Yummy.
