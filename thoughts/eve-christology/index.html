Eve Christology: Christ as Lover and Mother

Posted on June 12, 2026

While there has been much ink spilt on Christ the new Adam and Mary the new Eve, I've begun to feel like that is too theologically safe and we need to queer the binary. So, you can imagine my shock when watching a Stephen Morrison video on T. F. Torrance's theology, an extremely provocative Biblical reference was made. Speaking of Christ's incarnation as human, Stephen said (paraphrasing Torrance?) that he became bone of our bone, flesh of our flesh. Here is a reading of Christ/Eve for humans/Adam!

Who is Eve to Adam? She is his Lover, she is the Mother of all, she is his Helper, she is the one who sings for. Let us see who she is for Adam in the narrative of Genesis so that we may learn who Christ is for us.

It is not right for Adam to be alone, so God fashioned the woman. God did not see it fit for humanity to be abandoned children, so He appointed it right to incarnate as Christ.
The woman was appointed to be a helper (עֵ֖זֶר) for Adam. The divine title of עֵ֖זֶר is a direct link to Christ, the one who saves us from enslavement to Sin as prophetically revealed in the Psalms.
Adam was put into a deep sleep while God formed the woman. Humanity was consigned by God to enslavement to Sin, bound in a dreary state, while we anticipated the advent of the Christ.
Eve was fashioned out of the very bones and flesh of Adam by God. Christ was incarnate out of the bones and flesh of Mary (who typologically is Adam!) by the Spirit.
The story serves as an etiology for man leaving father and mother and clinging to his wife. Sinful humanity leaves their father the Devil and clings to Christ to whom they are wed.
The man and woman were both naked and not ashamed. There is no shame being bound to Christ as we have nothing that we can hide from Christ, and he does not hide anything from us.
Only after she is cursed does Adam name her Eve, the mother of all living. After entering into our cursed state, Christ redeems us and through his wounds we are born to new life and we name Him Saviour.

Christ as the divine feminine is not some new concept in Christian theology, in fact it is embedded directly into the pages of the New Testament as Paul calls Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God. What is the Wisdom of God, but the divine Mother operating along with God since the beginning of creation calling her children to wisdom throughout the Proverbs. Here we see Christ the New Eve, the divine Mother, who is our creator and saviour from evil. How about Christ the New Eve, the divine [feminine] Lover? We can see her appear to us in the Song of Songs. The woman's appearance was culturally undesireable, as she says: "Do not gaze at me because I am dark, because the sun has gazed on me". Christ came to us as one whose appearance was not desireable, as the prophet says: "He had no form or majesty that we should look at him, nothing in his appearance that we should desire him". The man says of the woman that she is the One for him: "My dove, my perfect one (אַחַ֥ת), is the only one (אַחַ֥ת), the darling of her mother, flawless to her who bore her". And the opening of the Law by Moses begins with the declaration that the Lord is One for us: "Hear, O Israel: The Lord is our God, the Lord alone (אֶחָֽד, the same word in a different case)."

This is not a unique position in the history of Christian thought, to see the divine feminine in Christ. We see this in pop up in the medieval mystic tradition with Julian of Norwich:

It is a characteristic of God to overcome evil with good. Jesus Christ therefore, who himself overcame evil with good, is our true Mother. We received our "Being" from Him and this is where His Maternity starts. And with it comes the gentle Protection and Guard of Love which will never ceases to surround us. Just as God is our Father, so God is also our Mother... Our highest Father, God Almighty, who is "Being", has always known us and loved us: because of this knowledge, through his marvellous and deep charity and with the unanimous consent of the Blessed Trinity, He wanted the Second Person to become our Mother, our Brother, our Saviour. It is thus logical that God, being our Father, be also our Mother. Our Father desires, our Mother operates and our good Lord the Holy Ghost confirms; we are thus well advised to love our God through whom we have our being, to thank him reverently and to praise him for having created us and to pray fervently to our Mother, so as to obtain mercy and compassion, and to pray to our Lord, the Holy Ghost, to obtain help and grace.
— Julian of Norwich, Revelations of Divine Love

We also have illuminated manuscripts and prayer books that depict Christ's wound as a vulva, depicting both the male and female sides of the divine. I hope this exercise of experimental allegorical reading opens up the theological potentials available in Scripture.
