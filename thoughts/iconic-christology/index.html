Iconic Christology: Dan and Dave on Jesus

Posted on April 22, 2026

Dan McClellan insists that the earliest levels of Christology envisioned Jesus like an idol that is location of the presence of YHWH on Earth and is emphatic that this is not saying that Jesus is God. However, I think there is a latent assumption here that humanity and divinity are two separate categories, which is a binary broken down by David Bentley Hart who argues quite persuasively that the Logos is the centre of being for all humans, and that Jesus was completely transparent to the Logos — as in one who uniquely is the presence of the deity like an idol. As much as Dan does not like Trinitarian theology, I think that his perspective is pretty well anticipated by a panentheist, monistic Christology á la Dave.

Colossians 1.15-19 (David Bentley Hart's second translation): ...[the Son] is the image of the invisible God, firstborn of all creation, because in him were created all things in the heavens and on earth, the visible as well as the invisible (whether Thrones or Lordships or Archons or Powers); all things were created through him and for him; and he is before all things, and all things hold together in him, and he is the head of the body, of the assembly—who is the origin, firstborn from the dead, so that he might himself hold first place in all things—for in him all the Fullness was pleased to take up a dwelling...

Dan argues that Jesus is portrayed as the angel of YHWH and a walking, breathing idol of YHWH in the New Testament, and that certainly has strong evidence throughout the New Testament. Idols function as a presencing of the deity without themselves being the deity directly.

... the Christian scriptures are not including Jesus within the “unique identity” of the deity of Israel, they are literarily asserting his endowment with the divine name, enabling him—as with the messenger of YHWH—to exercise divine power and to be both identified with and distinguished from that deity... The Christian authors are much more circumspect, satisfied to present a messianic figure who was more clearly compartmentalized from the deity, but enjoyed an ambiguous relationship with them that facilitated access to the necessary power and authority through the deity's agency, communicated via the name... the conceptual template that facilitated the messenger of YHWH's unique and ambiguous relationship with YHWH was the most intuitive and proximate way to represent Jesus's relationship to divinity.
— Dan McClellan, YHWH's Divine Images: A Cognitive Approach

I think that this approach is more than satisfying in explaining how it is the New Testament talks about Jesus becoming the Son of God (at resurrection for Paul in Romans 1.4) and being given the divine name (again in the resurrection for Paul in Philippians 2.9-11) and being one who presences God fully (as seen in Colossians as quoted).

To provide a mild apologia (and I shudder to use the word) for the development of the tradition to elevate the relationship of Jesus and God to one of co-substantiality with the Father by the time of the ecunemical councils, I don't think Dan's analysis is incongruent with a Nicene faith. I do think it to be completely incongruent with a theology that enforces a Creator-created distinction. But if we insist on a panenthestic and monistic relationship between God and the world then there is no problem at all in acknowledging this.

But orthodox Christology makes sense only when we think of the eternal Son not as some discrete individual ego who in eternity happens to instantiate an abstract divine nature alongside two other such discrete egos and who then in “personal” isolation from those other two assumes a human guise by also instantiating an abstract human nature. Jesus is no one other than the Son not because he is the incarnate psychological personality of the Logos; God does not have a psychology as such, or for that matter a soul. God did not become a man through pathos or change. Rather, God became human by Jesus being from the very first a man always wholly become God—which is also to say, wholly become the perfection of the human in its only natural end... And it seems obvious to me that the central affirmations of orthodox Christology must indicate just this: in Jesus, unlike us, there is none of the duality of identity that separates us from our only origin and end, no extrinsic liaison with or merely partial hospitality to the divine, but only the perfect unity of his “I am” with that of the eternal Father's utterance of the Logos in the living fullness of the Spirit. So perfect is this union, in fact, that even the resurrection was merely natural for him; there was no one in him whom death could conquer, for he was no one other than the one who has the power to lay down his soul and take it up again.
— David Bentley Hart, The Light of Tabor: Towards a Monistic Christology

Dave's argument, drawing heavily on Maximus, is that human-ness is not distinct from divine-ness. Rather, to be human is to be one who is transforming into divinity. For the average person, we have a mental illusion that we are distinct psychological agents separate from God. But Christ always was and is one who did not have this illusion. He is one who is always transparent to the Logos (God's animating principle in the world) that dwells within all of us. He uniquely shared in the name and authority of God because He fully revealed God. Christ is not some divine Superman descended from the clouds, like Zeus taking on limited appearances. He was, is, and will always be the full revelation of God in our midst who shows us identically who the Father is because when we look at Him all we can see is the Father.

This is a really important point to harp on. Jesus was not special because He wasn't like us, but special because He revealed exactly who we all are and fully can be. This has a direct ethical implication. One of my major concerns with the divinity of Christ is that it directly contradicts with Jesus as an ethical role model. Consider here the words of Dorothy Day.

"Don't call me a saint,” she is often said to have said. “I don't want to be dismissed that easily.”.. “When they call you a saint,” she said, “it means basically that you are not to be taken seriously.”... What Dorothy certainly opposed—and what saint wouldn't?—was being put on a pedestal, fitted to some pre-fab conception of holiness that would strip her of her humanity and, at the same time, dismiss the radical challenge of the gospel. “Dorothy Day could do such things (live in poverty, feed the hungry, go to jail for the cause of peace). She's a saint.” For those who said this sort of thing, the implication was that such actions—which would be out of reach for ordinary folk—must have come easily for her. She had no patience for that kind of cop-out.
— James Martin, S.J., Don't Call Me a Saint?

If canonizing a person who died in 1980 as a saint is grounds for people to dismiss them as an impossible model to follow, how much more of an impediment is it for people to follow Jesus' life and legacy as one who was ultimately self-sacrifical and endlessly forgiving of His enemies? If Jesus has been divinized in a way that is completely inaccessibe to us measely humans, then there is no reason for us to even think about living a life and dying a death like His. But, if Christ is the full revelation of what it means to be human by showing us that the Logos at centre of His being is the same Logos at the centre of our own beings, then we can actually follow His example and ethical teachings without dismissing them as "ideals" or "something that we can never achieve". See how Christ prays for us to have the exact type of indwelling with the Father as He has.

John 17.20-23, 25-26 (David Bentley Hart's second translation): But I make supplication not for them only, but also for those having faith in me on account of their word, that all may be one, just as you, Father, are in me and I in you, that they too might be [one] in us, so that the cosmos may have faith that you sent me forth. And I have given to them the glory you have given me, that they may be one just as we are one: I in them and you in me, that they might be brought to completion in one, so that the cosmos might know that you sent me forth, and loved them just as you loved me... but I knew you, and these know you have sent me forth; and your name I have made known, and will make known to them, so that the love with which you loved me may be in them, and I in them also.

Jesus prays for those who recognize His teachings and His identity as one who was sent by the Father to make the Father's name and glory known (just like an idol) to share in the same glory that He has from the Father. How is that possible unless the truth about humanity unless there is no separation between divinity and humanity. How can we even pretend to articulate that God is unchanging and that God became human unless humanity always was something divine. We may be under the illusion, as articulated in the myth of Adam and Eve, that divinity is something that we don't have. But the truth about human beings is that we are to be divinized, to become uncreated as argued by Maximus.

A concern that I have had for the past bit since ruminating on this model of Christology is whether the emphasis on Christ as fully revelatory of God is idolatry or not. And I don't think it is. I think that Jean-Luc Marion provides a helpful way of drawing a distinction between idols and icons.

When a philosophical thought expresses a concept of what it then names "God," this concept functions exactly as an idol. It gives itself to be seen, but thus all the better conceals itself as the mirror where thought, invisibly, has its forward point fixed, so that the invisable finds itself, with an aim suspended by the fixed concept, disqualified and abandoned; thought freezes, and the idolatrous concept of "God" appears, where, more than God, thought judges itself... The icon does not result from a vision but provokes one. The icon is not seen, but appears, or more originally seems, looks like... Whereas the idol results from the gaze that aims at it, the icon summons sight in letting the visible be saturated little by little with the invisible... The idol places its of center of gravity in a human gaze; thus, dazzled as it may be by the brilliance of the divine, the gaze still remains in possession of the idol, its solitary master... The icon, which unbalances human sight in order to engulf it in infinite depth, marks such an advance of God that even in times of the worst distress indifference cannot ruin it. For, to give itself to be seen, the icon needs only itself.
— Jean-Luc Marion, God Without Being

Marion is so complex and there is much in his work that I do not understand. But if I do understand him here, the distinction between an idol and icon is its relationship to human gaze. Icons are like gravitational wells that draw in all light and vision into themselves, whereas icons always operate transparently as signs who point to the greater, invisible reality that cannot be seen. If a concept or object or person claims to subsume our concept of God, drawing in our gaze to an exclusive relationship, it can rightly be called idol because it is trying to emulate everything that God is. (Now interestingly, Marion does not deny an idol's ability to presence God - he just points out that idols are limited in their abilities and have an expiration date). Icons, however, cause the human gaze to peer into infinity: to drink, become drunk, and drown in who God is beyond our conceptions of who God is. I cannot think of a better description of Christ who constantly is pointing to His Father, constantly described as one whom operates in pure, transparent tandem with the Father's will. Dave's describe of Christ's transparency to the Logos has much weight here. When we look at Christ, we cannot unable Him as a distinct agent, but as a window to God.

I think this also has a lot of implications for inter-faith discussions. As I discussed before, when we adopt a monistic Christology, we are able to accommodate all sorts of belief systems. If we take Dan's insistence that the New Testament does not directly name Jesus as God, but rather as one who presences God, Dave's transparent Christology, and Marion's idol/image distinction, I think that we can find much parity with religious thought writ large. "Jesus" as a concept is a conceptual idol for many, many Christians whose gaze on Christ does not peer through into the Father, but instead is stuck on their concepts of Jesus as a descending Zeus figure that one must uniquely affirm and deny all others. Of course, one can hold that they the only icon of God that they can find is Christ, but this cannot come from an a priori denial of all others, but rather the experience of how one's gaze is directed. I just think we have to be honest with ourselves. Are we consumed in the idol's crushing gravitational pull, doomed to its twilight and destruction by Nietzsche's hammer? Or are we looking at an icon of the invisible God who is revealed in Christ as agape in community (to invoke Marion's ultimate conclusion)?

I hope that I've shown how changing our philosophical framework from what's popular in Western circles to panentheism and monism we can 100% accommodate Dan's concerns on Christology. We do have to give up any notions of inheriting what has been handed down to us by the tradition, but if we are willing to move with the Spirit then this should be no problem to us.

In this sense, the living tradition, if indeed it is living, is essentially apocalyptic: an originating disruption of the historical past remembered in light of God's final disruption of the historical (and cosmic) future. One might even conclude that the tradition reveals its secrets only through moments of disruption precisely because it is itself, in its very essence, a disruption: it began entirely as a novum, an unanticipated awakening to something hitherto unknown that then requires the entirety of history to interpret… This is the only true faithfulness to the memory of an absolute beginning, a sudden unveiling without precise precedent: an empty tomb, say, or the voice of God heard in rolling thunder, or the descent of the Spirit like a storm of wind or tongues of fire. In a very real sense, the tradition exists only as a sustained apocalypse, a moment of pure awakening preserved as at once an ever dissolving recollection and an ever renewed surprise.
— David Bentley Hart, Tradition and Apocalypse: An Essay on the Future of Christian Belief

I fear Christianity and its apologists are too brittle to adapt to the future of belief. Hopefully those of us who sojourn in exile are able to preserve through into a bright, new dawn post-conservatism. Maybe one day Christology will evolve past the stale definitions that have been baby-birded to us from the councils through the sludge of Western thought.
