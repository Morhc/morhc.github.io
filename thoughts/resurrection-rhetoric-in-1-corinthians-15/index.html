Resurrection Rhetoric in 1 Corinthians 15

Posted on December 17, 2025

I've been thinking a lot about resurrection in 1 Corinthians.

1 Corinthians 15.3-19 For I handed on to you as of first importance what I in turn had received: that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the scriptures and that he was buried and that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the scriptures and that he appeared to Cephas, then to the twelve. Then he appeared to more than five hundred brothers and sisters at one time, most of whom are still alive, though some have died. Then he appeared to James, then to all the apostles. Last of all, as to one untimely born, he appeared also to me. For I am the least of the apostles, unfit to be called an apostle, because I persecuted the church of God. But by the grace of God I am what I am, and his grace toward me has not been in vain. On the contrary, I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I but the grace of God that is with me. Whether then it was I or they, so we proclaim and so you believed. Now if Christ is proclaimed as raised from the dead, how can some of you say there is no resurrection of the dead? If there is no resurrection of the dead, then Christ has not been raised, and if Christ has not been raised, then our proclamation is in vain and your faith is in vain. We are even found to be misrepresenting God, because we testified of God that he raised Christ—whom he did not raise if it is true that the dead are not raised. For if the dead are not raised, then Christ has not been raised. If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile, and you are still in your sins. Then those also who have died in Christ have perished. If for this life only we have hoped in Christ, we are of all people most to be pitied.

Apologists like to point to this passage for three reasons: (1) to gain some rhetorical power by pointing to a falsification point of the Christian belief system (2) to keep people in line who may be having doubts about the resurrection (3) to have some "early attestation" that corrobates the gospels. But why is Paul writing this passage to a group of Christians in Corinth?

Let's start with Paul's structure. The letter to the Corinthians is written as disciplinary of the hearsay Paul has heard about the Christian community in Corinth. It's not clear to me whether any of the concerns Paul has actually were happening. In 1.11 he says "For it has been made clear to me by Chloe's people..." and in 5.1 he says "It is actually reported...". This is the language of gossiping, and it is clear that Paul has been distant from the community for a while. When he came to them, he "laid a foundation" (3.10), and since leaving he's sent Timothy (4.17) and at least one (instructional? disciplinary?) letter prior (5.9). So, Paul's understanding of the Corinthian community is filtered now through the voice of Chloe's people, whoever they are and their gripes.

So the gripes of Chloe's people are that enough people in the (claimed to be highly sectarian) Corinthian community seem to believe that Jesus had been raised from the dead, but that there will not be a general resurrection. This claim, on the face of it, seems strange, but there is some sense to it. The idea of a resurrection at all comes from a Pharisaic Jewish theology that really focused in on Second and Third Isaiah along with second and first century BCE texts like Daniel and Maccabees and Jubilees and whatever else to argue that God will bring justice to all the strifes the Jewish people have faced at the hands of Empire, and particularly the Roman Empire, and establish an eternal soveriegn Jewish Empire.

Isaiah 60.1-5 Arise, shine, for your light has come, and the glory of the Lord has risen upon you. For darkness shall cover the earth and thick darkness the peoples, but the Lord will arise upon you, and his glory will appear over you. Nations shall come to your light and kings to the brightness of your dawn. Lift up your eyes and look around; they all gather together; they come to you; your sons shall come from far away, and your daughters shall be carried in their nurses' arms. Then you shall see and be radiant; your heart shall thrill and rejoice, because the abundance of the sea shall be brought to you; the wealth of the nations shall come to you.
Haggai 2.6-8 For thus says the Lord of hosts: Once again, in a little while, I will shake the heavens and the earth and the sea and the dry land, and I will shake all the nations, so that the treasure of all nations will come, and I will fill this house with splendor, says the Lord of hosts. The silver is mine, and the gold is mine, says the Lord of hosts. The latter splendor of this house shall be greater than the former, says the Lord of hosts, and in this place I will give prosperity, says the Lord of hosts.”
Daniel 7.11-18, 27 And as I watched, the beast was put to death and its body destroyed and given over to be burned with fire. As for the rest of the beasts, their dominion was taken away, but their lives were prolonged for a season and a time. As I watched in the night visions, I saw one like a human being coming with the clouds of heaven. And he came to the Ancient One and was presented before him. To him was given dominion and glory and kingship, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him. His dominion is an everlasting dominion that shall not pass away, and his kingship is one that shall never be destroyed. As for me, Daniel, my spirit was troubled within me, and the visions of my head terrified me. I approached one of the attendants to ask him the truth concerning all this. So he said that he would disclose to me the interpretation of the matter: “As for these four great beasts, four kings shall arise out of the earth. But the holy ones of the Most High shall receive the kingdom and possess the kingdom forever—forever and ever.”... The kingship and dominion and the greatness of the kingdoms under the whole heaven shall be given to the people of the holy ones of the Most High; their kingdom shall be an everlasting kingdom, and all dominions shall serve and obey them.

Unfortunately, because of evangelical fundamentalism, people are too quick to read these as prophecies about Jesus and find some allegorical meaning from these passages. And I mean, in part this is because these texts were being constantly re-interpreted because they were really banking on the Maccabees winning in some ultimate sense over Rome and having the Jewish Empire be ushered in by plundering the wealth of Rome (just as the Egyptians were plundered in the Exodus narrative I assume, see Exodus 12.35-36). Anyways, to get back on topic, the Pharisaic theology expected this Jewish Empire inaugurated by God via resurrecting the dead, but the Sadducees rejected this entire theological project. In part because they were cooperating with the Roman authorities, so their theology had to be one of Roman Empire, but also because they had no theological basis to believe in a resurrection. As religious conservatives, they only affirmed the books of Moses and rejected all later texts as spurious (see Matthew 22.23-33 to see some interaction there).

Most relevant for Corinth is the Greek philosophical context, which rejected the idea of the resurrection of the body, because physical bodies are subject to change and decay. So to be raised to an eternal existence in a body is to eternally be trapped in a state of decay, rather than ascending to the heavens as that unchanging part of ourselves of soul/spirit. These concerns of the obvious issues of Christian resurrection as the final state can be seen in Gregory of Nyssa's On the Soul and the Resurrection. And perhaps the Corinthians were also dealing with reconciling that Christians around them were dying despite being in Christ (similar to the community in Thessalonika, see 1 Thessalonians, and this would explain the comments about baptism for the dead in v. 29 + not all of us [meaning Paul and people he knew] dying, and trumpets raising the dead in v. 51-52).

So we are supposed to think that some among the Corinthians rejected a general resurrection picture, perhaps for the reason of the Saduccees or Greek philosophy, but did believe in the resurrection of Jesus. I think this makes little sense, and Paul leverges this incongruity to make his case in 15.12-19 and goes on to wax poetic on the qualia of the resurrected body to assuage any concerns about eternally existing as a rotting corpse. But let's really try to peer through Paul's rhetoric and figure out what's going on in Corinth.

  1. The anti-general resurrection Corinthians believed Jesus had to been raised, but hadn't thought far enough ahead to work out the implications of denying the general resurrection.
  2. Those who disputed resurrection in Corinth believed that Jesus was translated/ascended to the Father, but not that His physical body had been raised. Perhaps they believed that His body disappeared like Romulus. Or maybe they didn't care about His body at all (there is no mention of an empty tomb by Paul famously).
  3. Our Corinthian troublemakers didn't just not believe in the resurrection, but also disbelieved the ascendency. Perhaps this is a group of Christians who followed Jesus teachings as a way of life and had ecstatic experiences, but were not interested in the crucifixion and resurrection bits.

If I had to place a bet, it would be the second scenario. So what is Paul doing in 1 Corinthians 15.3-19? He's strong arming those who don't believe with a chain of authority and a complete dismissal of their beliefs as stupid. I know people think that maybe Paul is citing an early catechetical formula/creed here, but honestly I don't see it. It's too convenient for evangelical apologists for that to be true, and it feels too much like retrogecting concepts of councils and binding doctrines. You have to start from where the argument is going to see why Paul starts off the chapter like this.

  1. Paul has had an ecstatic vision of the risen Christ while tweaking out in the desert or something (see Galatians 1, the Acts narrative has little to do with history).
  2. This leads to the belief that God has overcome Sin and Death's grip on humanity by sidestepping the Law (check out my post on Romans as theodicy and my breakdown of his atonement theology to understand how I think Paul's soteriology works).
  3. This overcoming has to be in a body received at the general resurrection because of Paul's Pharisaic theology.
  4. However, the Jewish understanding of a bodily resurrection doesn't jive at all with contemporary Greek wisdom (and interestingly Paul doesn't whip out his rhetoric from chapters 1 and 2 to censor this way of thinking).
  5. So, we must be raised to an incorruptible body, and therefore one that is not flesh and blood (contra Luke's later apologetic framing of the risen Jesus who explicitly says that He is flesh and blood).
  6. The resurrection must be general because of this Pharisaic framing, but also because the whole buisness of overcoming the Law.
  7. Therefore, for Paul's solution to theodicy to work (once again, I think this is all very much rooted in how Romans is constructed and Paul's atonement theology lynchpinned around Romans 4 saying that we are justified by resurrection) and for Gentiles to be considered Christians, he needs a concept of general resurrection.
  8. Therefore, he creates this chain of authority saying that all these important Christian leaders and even five hundred unnamed people saw Jesus raised from the dead.

Why even include this whole bit in v. 3-11 if the issue was not believing in a general resurrection? That's another drop in the bucket for those in Christians in Corinth not even believing that Jesus was raised from the dead. So to get back to the open question as how this passage is being used by Paul. I think it's very much to rhetorically beat down any Christians who did not need any concept of a resurrection. Maybe they were Platonists who believed that our souls rested in God. Maybe they had a completely realized eschatology that took the kingdom of God as a serious ethical command, but just didn't need a concept of an afterlife. In 1 Corinthians 16.3 Paul does ask them to set aside money for the church in Jerusalem , perhaps this establishes some sort of link between the Corinthians and the Ebionite Christian movement (which was potentially led by James and later by sons and nephews who carried on the Jesus dynasty). It's impossible to say anything really beyond speculation. The claim that faith is absolutely futile and that Christians who don't believe in a resurrection are to be pitied for how sorrowful they are just doesn't seem to hold water. Once again, we are forced to muse and wish we knew more about the fascinating early Christianties and ways of following Jesus beyond what was sanctioned by third and fourth century virgin men who aligned themselves with the Roman Empire.

What this passage isn't, however, is early attestation to a creedal formula that Jesus was raised from the dead that can be dated to within 5 years of the event. It is also not proof of a chain of eyewitness testimony. It is also not a basis for saying that a Christianity that rejects a resurrected Jesus has no place in theological discourse. And finally, 1 Corinthians 15 is not evidence for essential Christian belief in a physical, bodily re-animated Jesus. Paul rejects that flat out and argues for belief in a strangely transformed Jesus that has passed from the realm of physical to spiritual, from seed to wheat, from earthly to stellar glory, from dust to heaven, from a ψυχικόν body to a πνευματικόν body. ψυχικόν is translated in 15.44 as "natural" and πνευματικόν as "spiritual" in the NRSVue, but see how David Bentley Hart translates it.

1 Corinthians 15.42-50 (David Bentley Hart's second edition): “Thus also the resurrection of the dead: it is sown in perishability, it is raised in imperishability; it is sown in dishonor, it is raised in glory; it is sown in weakness, it is raised in power; it is sown a psychical body, it is raised a spiritual body.ae If there is a psychical body, there is also a spiritual. So it has also been written, “The first man Adam came to be a living soul,” and the last Adam a life-making spirit. But not the spiritual first, but rather the psychical, the spiritual thereafter. The first man out of the earth—earthly; the second man [the Lord] out of heaven. As the earthly man, so also those who are earthly; and, as the heavenly, so also those who are heavenly; and, just as we have borne the image of the earthly man, we shall also bear the image of the heavenly man. And I say this, brothers: that flesh and blood cannot inherit the Kingdom of God; neither does perishability inherit imperishability.

David contrasts psychical with spiritual instead of "natural", and this is pretty important because it actually is connected to Paul's arguments in the opening chapters of the letter. Look at this excerpt from the second chapter.

1 Corinthians 2.13-15 (David Bentley Hart's second edition): Which things we also speak not in words taught from human wisdom, but rather in those taught by a [Holy] Spirit, combining things spiritual with things spiritual. But a Psychical man does not receive the things of God's Spirit; for to him it is folly, and he is unable to know them, since they are discerned spiritually. The Spiritual man, moreover, discerns all things, yet is discerned by no one.

So it's clear to me that the Pauline view is definitely not that Jesus was raised in some bodily sense like how most evangelicals think. Paul actually may be arguing more of a realized eschatology than many realize. See the footnote provided by David on translating psychical and spiritual.

In these verses, 14-15, an antithesis crucial to Paul's larger argument, especially in chapter fifteen, makes its first appearance: that between “psychical” life (which comes from psychē or, in Latin, anima: hence also “animate” or “animal” life) and “pneumatic” or “spiritual” life (which is of a radically different nature). The term “Psychical man” is a rendering of ψυχικός (psychikos), while the term “Spiritual man” is a rendering of πνευματικός (pnevmatikos): not “spiritual” in the vague sense in which we use that term, but referring to a special condition—and perhaps special status within the early church—probably that of someone filled with and transformed by God's Spirit, and so living according to pnevma rather than psychē.”

Paul is arguing something quite distinct here: because Jesus was raised to an incorruptible life in God, transformed and animated by the Spirit, we ought to live a life emulating that. Also, we have to note that Paul does not believe in bodies being raised from the ground in a literal sense seeing how he said flesh and blood will not inherit the kingdom of God (15.50). I actually can't emphasize this enough because it's easy to harmonize all references to resurrection as bodies emerging from the grave.

Jubilees 23.30-31: And at that time the Lord will heal His servants, And they shall rise up and see great peace, And drive out their adversaries. And the righteous shall see and be thankful, And rejoice with joy for ever and ever, And shall see all their judgments and all their curses on their enemies. And their bones shall rest in the earth, And their spirits shall have much joy, And they shall know that it is the Lord who executes judgment, And shows mercy to hundreds and thousands and to all that love Him

Jubilees is written probably around 150 BC and features a non-physical resurrection. The saints shall rise up and see things and be thankful and their bones shall rest in the earth. Let's see a contemporary text to Jubilees in Daniel which was probably written within 10 years.

Daniel 12.2-3: Many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake, some to everlasting life and some to shame and everlasting contempt. Those who are wise shall shine like the brightness of the sky, and those who lead the many to righteousness, like the stars forever and ever.

Is Daniel describing a physical resurrection? It's not clear at all to me, and in fact I would lean against it. Paul uses the same language of "dust of the earth" and "glory of the stars" and clearly refutes the idea of a physical resurrection (even though he is constantly saying "raised from the dead"). Ezekiel 37 and Isaiah 26.19 also don't refer to physical resurrections, but metaphorical national rebirth. Also as a quick overview elsewhere:

  • 2 Maccabees: it seems 7.11-14 does describe a physical reconstitution
  • Mark: unclear if he believes in a physical resurrection, there is no appearance
  • Matthew: mixed because of the saints being raised physically but Jesus seems like a phantasm
  • Luke: a physical resurrection that directly contradicts Paul (see that I am flesh and blood, requests they touch Him, Jesus eats fish)
  • John: it seems he does, but he actually has some ambiguity (requests to touch but doesn't say that He is flesh and blood, only implies that He eats fish)
  • 1 Peter: it's not clear what is meant in 1.3, but looking at "suffering in the flesh" (4.1) vs "might live in the spirit as God does" (4.6) it seems that this describes a spiritual resurrection, not a physical one.
  • Revelation: a mess of symbols that can be interpreted however one wants

Anyways, sorry to get off topic, but the point is that Paul is responding to an early group of Christians who do not believe in the resurrection and he implores them to belive in a spiritual resurrection, which is later harmonized with the gospels to be a physical resurrection. So any apologetic arguments that want to use 1 Corinthian 15 as a basis for credible eye witness testimony and an insistence that one must affirm a bodily resurrection to be a Christian are just wrong. This should also shape how one read 1 Thessalonians 4.13-18 and Romans 8.11. These texts cannot be describing the body raising from the dead 1 Thessalonians is probably describing something like Jubilees 23, and Romans 8 is clearly using realized eschatological language of being raised now (hence "to your mortal body"). Anyways anyways, to conclude, Paul's rhetoric on resurrection in 1 Corinthians 15 does not reflect an early creedal form of belief, nor does it provide evidence for a physical body coming out of a grave. Paul probably would not care if Jesus' body was rotting, it has no relevancy to his theology seeing how he has no care for a Christ of the flesh but of the spirit (2 Corinthians 5.16).
