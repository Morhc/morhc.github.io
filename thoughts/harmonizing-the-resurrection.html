Harmonizing the Resurrection

Posted on February 2, 2026

`

Apologists will claim that the gospels are four different perspectives of the same event, and that if you just pay attention to the narrative details, they will be perfectly harmonizable. Now, I don't think that's true at all, but let's grant it and actually just do it. Time to repeat Tatian's error of creating the Diatessaron for the discovery of the empty tomb. I will reproduce all of Mark's gospel as the base text since it was written first and leave out non-consequential differences.

And very early on the first day of the week, when the sun had risen, they went to the tomb. Mark repeats here, making sure to contradict John.
They had been saying to one another, “Who will roll away the stone for us from the entrance to the tomb?” When they looked up, they saw that the stone, which was very large, had already been rolled back. And suddenly there was a great earthquake, for an angel of the Lord, descending from heaven, came and rolled back the stone and sat on it. His appearance was like lightning and his clothing white as snow. For fear of him the guards shook and became like dead men. They found the stone rolled away from the tomb, but when they went in they did not find the body. and saw that the stone had been removed from the tomb. Well here we find the narratives start to drastically diverge. Mark's women have the stone rolled away silently, Matthew's spectacularly, and Luke and John's stone is pre-rolled. Also Matthew adds Roman guards to the tomb. We will return in John in a bit, but note that he does not describe any woman entering the tomb.

Remember how he told you, while he was still in Galilee, that the Son of Man must be handed over to the hands of sinners and be crucified and on the third day rise again.” Was it one man, a scary angel, or two dazzling men who told them Jesus was raised? What did they tell the women?
But go, tell his disciples and Peter that he is going ahead of you to Galilee; there you will see him, just as he told you.” Then go quickly and tell his disciples, 'He has been raised from the dead, and indeed he is going ahead of you to Galilee; there you will see him.' This is my message for you.” Luke does not recount that the angels tell the women to tell the others.
So they went out and fled from the tomb, for terror and amazement had seized them, and they said nothing to anyone, for they were afraid. So they left the tomb quickly with fear and great joy and ran to tell his disciples. Now it was Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary the mother of James, and the other women with them who told this to the apostles. Or, as is the case with John (welcome back), did Mary Magdalene (who curiously says "we" despite being the only mentioned, perhaps a Johannine editorial mistake) return and say that the body is missing? Mark ends his narrative here famously, but we can continue with the others.
Suddenly Jesus met them and said, “Greetings!” And they came to him, took hold of his feet, and worshiped him. Then Jesus said to them, “Do not be afraid; go and tell my brothers and sisters to go to Galilee; there they will see me.” Matthew alone recounts Jesus appearing to the women on their way to the disciples and ends this part of the narrative. However, John adds a personal visitation to Mary Magdalene alone right after Peter and the beloved disciple find it empty (John 20.11-18).
But these words seemed to them an idle tale, and they did not believe them. But Peter got up and ran to the tomb; stooping and looking in, he saw the linen cloths by themselves; then he went home, amazed at what had happened. Then Peter and the other disciple set out and went toward the tomb. The two were running together, but the other disciple outran Peter and reached the tomb first. He bent down to look in and saw the linen wrappings lying there, but he did not go in. Then Simon Peter came, following him, and went into the tomb. He saw the linen wrappings lying there, and the cloth that had been on Jesus's head, not lying with the linen wrappings but rolled up in a place by itself. Then the other disciple, who reached the tomb first, also went in, and he saw and believed, for as yet they did not understand the scripture, that he must rise from the dead. Then the disciples returned to their homes. And here we have the conclusions of Luke and John who both record a visitation to the empty tomb by Peter (and the beloved one by John who takes his own gaze after Peter).

These are just not minor narrative differences, but completely different stories about the same event. What are inerrantists wanting us to believe here? It makes absolutely no sense to try to harmonize these accounts. Something else that I want to add to this conversation is the account in the Gospel of Peter, which was likely written within 50 or so years of the canonical gospels.

Gospel of Peter v. 50-57 Now at the dawn of the Lord's Day Mary Magdalene, a female disciple of the Lord (who, afraid because of the Jews since they were inflamed with anger, had not done at the tomb of the Lord what women were accustomed to do for the dead beloved by them), having taken with her women friends, came to the tomb where he had been placed. And they were afraid lest the Jews should see them and were saying, 'If indeed on that day on which he was crucified we could not weep and beat ourselves, yet now at his tomb we may do these things. But who will roll away for us even the stone placed against the door of the tomb in order that, having entered, we may sit beside him and do the expected things? For the stone was large, and we were afraid lest anyone see us. And if we are unable, let is throw against the door what we bring in memory of him; let us weep and beat ourselves until we come to our homes.' And having gone off, they found the sepulcher opened. And having come forward, they bent down there and saw there a certain young man seated in the middle of the sepulcher, comely and clothed with a splendid robe, who said to them: 'Why have you come? Whom do you seek? Not that one who was crucified? He is risen and gone away. But if you do not believe, bend down and see the place where he lay, because he is not here. For he is risen and gone away to there whence he was sent.' Then the women fled frightened.

This account is something that can be harmonized, particularly with Mark's gospel. The only unique details are some comments about Mary's fear, some extra dialogue on the way to the tomb, and the extra detail that the young man is wearing a splendid robe and was comely. If this crazy early text can be harmonized with Mark and the other canonical accounts cannot, then what does that say about them? Certainly not that they are inerrant historical accounts from differing perspectives. Anyways, this was a fun exercise to do, and I hope you enjoyed it and realize that all conservative theologians are full of it for most (if not all) of their claims.
