Contra Ehrman

Posted on April 30, 2026

Over the past year or two, Bart Ehrman has been repeatedly talking about how early Christianity quickly abandoned the teachings of Jesus, and that this begins with Paul. Central to Jesus' teaching, Bart argues, is that God freely forgives human beings, but Paul asserts that God insists on a payment prior to forgiving. Christian theology went along with Paul and left Jesus behind, so he says.

This concept of forgiveness is not to be confused with “atonement,” although the terms are often muddled up, even among theologians and other thinkers. “Atonement” literally refers to a restored relationship in which two individuals (or groups) who were at odds become “at-one” (“at-one-ment”) because a price has been paid, a penalty assessed, or a punishment meted out. By its very nature, atonement is not freely given. It is a kind of retributive justice.
— Bart Ehrman, Love Thy Stranger: How the Teachings of Jesus Transformed the Moral Conscience of the West

I have a pretty big problem with Ehrman's thesis. I've not read the book, but I've listened to maybe three or four interviews he's done on this, and it really seems to me that Bart is taking the evangelical Protestant reading of Paul as the reading of Paul. The fact of the matter is that not all Pauline readers find atonement in his theology. Myself, for instance. I have argued that no (real or forged) Pauline text argues that Jesus paid our debts. Paul teaches that through the cross that our sin debts are cancelled and that there is reconciliation in humanity's relationship with God. That is the language of free forgiveness. I agree with Bart that retributive justice is incompatible with forgiveness, I also think it is incompatible with Pauline theology. Christ's death and resurrection frees humanity from the demands of the Law, not because He paid our debts but because He sidesteps the Law altogether (hence having died to the Law and no longer being under it). Christ restores our relationship with God who is endlessly forgiving by bringing us back into the fold, not because He paid off God to calm Him down. While Bart is correct to say that much of (contemporary) Christian theology completely misses the distinction between forgiveness and "atonement" (where he really means penal substitution), I think that he is off the mark to say that this is a fundamental contradiction between Jesus and Paul.
