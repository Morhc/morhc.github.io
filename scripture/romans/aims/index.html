AIMS OF THE EPISTLE TO THE ROMANS

The purpose of the epistle to the Romans is a complex question that has no consensus answer. It is clear from the structure of the epistle that Paul is trying to justify himself to those hearing it be read that his gospel message. He states that his apostolic authority to bring obedience to non-Jewish people comes from the resurrected Jesus (1.1-5). I think this is fulcrum on which the entire gospel that Paul proclaims turns as I've explained here.

Throughout the epistle to the Romans, Paul tries to answer a fundamental question: why haven't the gentiles come into obedience according to the Torah? and this is how to bring them to obedience! This was likely a general expectation among the Jewish people that took the prophetic texts as authoritative, such as Pharisees like Paul. Jewish apocalypticism expected that the world would come to a sudden end and a beginning of the reign of God's kingdom through the restoration and conquering of the nation of Israel to rule over all the other nations (eg. Haggai 2.6-8, Isaiah 60.1-5, Daniel 7.11-27; the Christian Scriptures reflect this same sentiment in Revelation though it is Christians who are conquerers over the Empire rather than Israel over gentile nations). One of the markers of the end was the general resurrection when all the righteous Jewish people are raised back to life. Paul must have expected these prophetic utterances to be coming true in his day because of the resurrection of Jesus (which is the firstfruits of his expectation of the soon coming harvest of resurrection). Therefore, the gentiles must be brought into submission under the Torah — but they clearly were not doing that on their own. Knowing his Scriptures, we must wonder what Paul was thinking when he read:

Jeremiah 1.4-5
Now the word of the Lord came to me saying,
“Before I formed you in the womb I knew you, and before you were born I consecrated you; I appointed you a prophet to the nations.”
Isaiah 42.1-4; 49.1-6, 22-23
Here is my servant, whom I uphold, my chosen, in whom my soul delights; I have put my spirit upon him; he will bring forth justice to the nations. He will not cry out or lift up his voice or make it heard in the street; a bruised reed he will not break, and a dimly burning wick he will not quench; he will faithfully bring forth justice. He will not grow faint or be crushed until he has established justice in the earth, and the coastlands wait for his teaching.

Listen to me, O coastlands; pay attention, you peoples from far away! The Lord called me before I was born; while I was in my mother's womb he named me. He made my mouth like a sharp sword; in the shadow of his hand he hid me; he made me a polished arrow; in his quiver he hid me away. And he said to me,
“You are my servant, Israel, in whom I will be glorified.”
But I said,
“I have labored in vain; I have spent my strength for nothing and vanity; yet surely my cause is with the Lord and my reward with my God.”
And now the Lord says, who formed me in the womb to be his servant, to bring Jacob back to him, and that Israel might be gathered to him, for I am honored in the sight of the Lord, and my God has become my strength—he says,
“It is too light a thing that you should be my servant to raise up the tribes of Jacob and to restore the survivors of Israel; I will give you as a light to the nations, that my salvation may reach to the end of the earth.”
Thus says the Lord God:
"I will soon lift up my hand to the nations and raise my signal to the peoples, and they shall bring your sons in their bosom, and your daughters shall be carried on their shoulders. Kings shall be your foster fathers and their queens your nursing mothers. With their faces to the ground they shall bow down to you and lick the dust of your feet. Then you will know that I am the Lord; those who wait for me shall not be put to shame."

Whom is the one that the word of the LORD has come to, appointing them to be a prophet to the nations. Whom is the light to the nations? As far as I can tell, Paul is not only reading his Scriptures but reading himself into his Scriptures. He sees this as his duty to help bring about the colonial and domineering eschatological desire of the prophets.

So if it is true for Paul that the time has come of obedience to the Torah of all the nations so that God's justice may be established, why does he talk of dying to the Law? I think it comes from him seeing that obedience to the Torah was simply not happening among the nations. Whether his initial gospel proclamation shortly after conversion tried to do this cannot be determined from any text-critical analysis, but one must imagine it to be so.

Romans 2.17-24 But if you call yourself a Jew and rely on the law and boast of your relation to God and know his will and determine what really matters because you are instructed in the law, and if you are sure that you are a guide to the blind, a light to those who are in darkness, a corrector of the foolish, a teacher of children, having in the law the embodiment of knowledge and truth, you, then, who teach others, will you not teach yourself? You who preach against stealing, do you steal? You who forbid adultery, do you commit adultery? You who abhor idols, do you rob temples? You who boast in the law, do you dishonor God by your transgression of the law? For, as it is written,
“The name of God is blasphemed among the gentiles because of you.”

If it was Paul, or someone he knew, he must have seen that demanding gentiles follow the Torah simply was not effectual. This is a really big theological problem for Paul: the Torah was understood to be how justice was established both interpersonally and cosmically. Further, if the prophets were wrong that would make God a liar.

Deuteronomy 4.5-8
See, just as the Lord my God has charged me, I now teach you statutes and ordinances for you to observe in the land that you are about to enter and occupy. You must observe them and perform them, for this will show your wisdom and discernment to the peoples, who, when they hear all these statutes, will say, 'Surely this great nation is a wise and discerning people!' For what other great nation has a god so near to it as the Lord our God is whenever we call to him? And what other great nation has statutes and ordinances as just as this entire law that I am setting before you today?

If the purpose of the Law has not been fulfilled and gentiles are not coming under obedience to the Law, how can it be that the general resurrection has started with Jesus? Here is where Paul innovates a blend of Hellenstic and Jewish theology to generate new theological concepts like the cosmic forces Sin and Death who have trapped humanity in their grasp. The fact that gentiles are not becoming obedient to the Law is not a failure of the Law, but these cosmic forces using it to ensnare humans (as described in Romans 7). How then are gentiles going to become obedient to God? This is where Paul goes to Habakkuk in the opening chapter of Romans.

Habakkuk 2.4
Look at the proud! Their spirit is not right in them, but the righteous live by their faithfulness.

Paul finds the scattered references in his Scriptures about obedience to God apart from the Law (such as Abraham in chapter 4) and uses it as a lynchpin to renegotiate the entire covenental structure of his Scriptures. Torah observance no longer remains as the way to be loyally obedient to God, because the cosmic forces have made the Torah a snare.

This framework has actually quite little to do with Jesus as Messiah in its approach, but instead becomes the moment in history when God begins to work this out. One may ask then if Jesus have anything to do with it beyond the signalling of a new paradigm? Jesus, for some reason, becomes the righteous one who upheld the Torah, died, and was raised free of the Torah. Perhaps Paul read Jesus similarly to subsequent generations of gnostic Christians: escape from the fleshly body held down by evil to a spiritual life joined to the pleroma.

Why is it that Jesus specifically went from crucified rebel to the divinized one for Paul? It's not like Jesus was particularly unique in his time, nor was Paul a follower of his. There were thousands and thousands of Jewish people crucified for like a century. There were dozens of failed Messiahs. Was it simply that Paul was having guilt-induced psychosis for his violence towards the first generation of Jesus followers? It is impossible to say. Whatever the reason is, Paul began having mystical visions of a Jesus raised to life by God. These mystical revelations become the entire basis for Paul for his theology. Perhaps he regularly practiced merkabah mysticism and saw the throne room of God (eg. 2 Corinthians 12). These visions fueled his assertions that the time of gentiles becoming obedient to God via the crucified and risen Jesus.

But why is he even bothering to send this epistle to the Christians in Rome? I think that it is because he is trying to convince them that they should endorse his message financially as he travels the Mediterrean (Romans 15.22-29). He wants to sleep over at someone's house as he continues spreading the gospel to the furthest of the coastlands in the Roman empire: Spain. He probably saw this as a critical step in fulfilling what was described in deutero-Isaiah.



A huge problem looms in the distance for this interpretation of Paul, however. What if the canonical form of the Pauline letters as we have them today are a product of second century editors trying to undermine the influence of Marcion as suggested by Markus Vinzent? If there was some collection of Pauline letters that was first collected and edited by Marcion and then rewritten and expanded by someone sympathetic to the theology of Tertullian and Irenaeus then we have to un-learn and re-learn who Paul was, who Marcion was, and who the second and third century proto-catholic theologians were. What if Nina E. Livesey is right that not only did the proto-catholics take over a Marcionite collection of letters, but that the Marcionite school actually wholesale created what we call the Pauline epistles? I honestly have no ability to interact with these lines of reasoning meaningfully. To these cutting-edge scholars, I sit and listen. Perhaps in the future I will be able to engage them seriously.
